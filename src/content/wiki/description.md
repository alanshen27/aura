# Project Description

## Simple Version

Mastitis is an infection of a cow's udder, usually caused by bacteria entering the udder. This disease presents significant obstacles to the dairy industry. Many cows develop infections, resulting in substantial financial losses for farmers. Most losses stem from decreased milk yield or diminished milk quality during infection, rather than from medication costs.

Detecting mastitis is challenging because early symptoms are subtle, and subclinical cases lack visible signs. Common diagnostic techniques include measuring milk somatic cell count or visually inspecting clots or discoloration. Detection methods are often expensive, and what is effective on one farm may fail on another.

Expenses for veterinary care and treatments escalate, especially with large herds. Mastitis is a pervasive issue demanding faster, more affordable detection and solutions so cows and farmers can thrive.

## DA BEEG BAD PROBLEM

Mastitis persists within herds, despite technological advances, costing billions of the milking industry. Despite technological advances in herd management and medicine, mastitis continues to affect both animal welfare and farm productivity. It is estimated that one-third of dairy cows contract mastitis during their lifetime, with annual infection rates ranging from 47–65%.

Mastitis is a massive economic burden for dairy farmers across the globe. Across the dairy industry, losses are estimated at around 30 billion euros annually, exhibiting the widespread and significant impact. Specifically, direct treatment expenses – such as veterinary care and antibiotics – account for less than 15% of total costs. Major financial losses stem from poor milk quality, reduced milking capacity, and additional labor costs to treat infected animals.

Mastitis often eludes detection by common methods. Subclinical mastitis can persist undetected while continuing to reduce milk quality and spread within a herd. Effective and reliable diagnosis requires specialized testing methods and continuous monitoring systems, posing another challenge for farmers and demanding additional investment in specialized personnel, all of which can cost farmers thousands at a time. Furthermore, the pathogenic diversity and the rich microbiome of farm environments require tailored diagnostic and treatment strategies for each farm, challenging the development of standardized solutions.

Treatment has significant upfront and ongoing expenses: veterinary consultations hover around €150, while diagnostic tests such as milk cultures (at an estimated price of €30 per sample), somatic cell count testing (€5 per sample), or PCR-based methods (€60 per test) quickly accumulate when applied at the herd level. Treatment adds an estimated €100 per case for antibiotics and supportive care, along with additional labor (€25 per case) and, in some cases, specialized consulting services (€120 per hour). These costs, combined with the need for repeated monitoring and expert intervention, make effective mastitis management both resource-intensive and difficult to scale. As costs accumulate, we lose billions of dollars, affecting farmers worldwide.

Mastitis represents not only a biological problem that needs to be addressed medically, but also a technological and logistical obstacle. The need for accessible, rapid, and precise diagnostics, as well as more efficient disease management strategies, is becoming paramount as the milking industry expands. Addressing mastitis in the early stages could significantly reduce economic losses, improve animal welfare, and enhance the sustainability of dairy production systems globally.
